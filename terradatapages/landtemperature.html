What is Land Surface Temperature?

Land surface temperature (LST) is how hot the "surface" of the Earth would feel to the touch in a particular location. From a satellite's point of view, the "surface" is whatever it sees when it looks through the atmosphere to the ground. It could be snow and ice, the grass on a lawn, the roof of a building, or the treetops in a forest. Thus, land surface temperature is not the same as the air temperature that is included in the daily weather report.

Understanding Temperature Anomalies

An anomaly is when the conditions depart from average conditions for a particular place at a given time of year. The maps show daytime land surface temperature anomalies for a given month compared to the average conditions during that period between 2000-2008. Places that were warmer than average are red, places that were near normal are white, and places that were cooler than average are blue. The observations were collected by the Moderate Resolution Imaging Spectroradiometer (MODIS) on NASA's Terra satellite.

Some land surface temperature anomalies are simply random weather phenomena, not part of a specific pattern or trend. Others anomalies are more meaningful. Widespread cold anomalies may be an indication of a harsh winter with lots of snow on the ground. Small, patchy warm anomalies that appear in forests or other natural ecosystems may indicate deforestation or insect damage. Many urban areas also show up as hot spots in these maps because developed areas are often hotter in the daytime than surrounding natural ecosystems or farmland. Warm anomalies that persist over large parts of the globe for many years can be signs of global warming.

HOW RISING TEMPERATURES AFFECT YOU & YOUR COMMUNITY

Rising land surface temperatures aren't just an environmental issue—they're a direct threat to your health, safety, and the wellbeing of your community. Here's how intensifying heat impacts daily life:

🏥

Heat-Related Illness

Extreme heat increases risks of heat exhaustion, heat stroke, and dehydration. Vulnerable populations like the elderly, children, and outdoor workers face the highest risks.

🌾

Agricultural Impacts

Higher temperatures reduce crop yields, increase water demand for irrigation, and threaten food security. Heat stress affects livestock health and productivity.

💧

Water Scarcity

Increased evaporation from reservoirs and soil moisture depletion leads to water shortages, affecting drinking water supplies and ecosystem health.

🏭

Energy Demand

Higher temperatures increase electricity demand for cooling, straining power grids and increasing energy costs, particularly during heatwaves.

SOLUTIONS & MITIGATION STRATEGIES

The trajectory shown by the data is reversible. Implementing changes at global, municipal, and individual levels can drastically reduce temperature rise and its impacts:

  1. Climate Action: Reducing greenhouse gas emissions is essential to slow global warming and limit temperature increases.
  2. Urban Planning: Implementing green infrastructure, cool roofs, and increasing vegetation to reduce urban heat island effects.
  3. Sustainable Agriculture: Adopting practices that improve soil health, increase water retention, and reduce temperature impacts.
  4. Energy Efficiency: Improving building insulation, promoting passive cooling, and transitioning to renewable energy sources.

What You Can Do Today

At Home

  • Plant trees and vegetation around your property
  • Install reflective or green roofs
  • Use energy-efficient cooling systems
  • Reduce energy consumption during peak hours

In Your Community

  • Advocate for urban greening initiatives
  • Support renewable energy projects
  • Participate in community cooling centers
  • Promote water conservation measures

The health of our planet is a reflection of our collective actions. Small changes, collectively, create a major global shift.
